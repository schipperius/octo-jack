The Gauls were a Celtic people of the La Tène culture, living in the region roughly corresponding to what is now France, Belgium and Switzerland.

They also lived in what today is part of the Netherlands, Germany and Italy.

The La Tène culture developed and flourished from about 450 BC until the Roman conquest in the 1st century BC.

The Gauls at this time were comprised of a confederation of tribes representing different ethnic groups that flourished across the Western European continent. 3


  • The region of Gaul.

    Illustration from John Haywood 4

The Gauls were a wealthy and prosperous people, with economic ties with Germanic Tribes north of the Rhine, and Greeks, Phoenicians and Romans to the south.

In 58 BC, Gaius Julius Caesar decided to conquer and plunder some territories to help get himself out of debt.

It is likely that Gaul was not his initial target, but that did not stop him from waging a series of military campaigns against several Gallic Tribes. 5


  • The wealth of Gaul.

    Photograph from the Institut National de Recherches Archéologiques Preventives 6

These Gallic wars as they became known, ended in 52 BC with a Roman victory in the Battle of Alesia, led by none other than Gaius Julius Caesar.

This was a significant military victory for the Romans, effectively ending the majority of Gallic resistance to Rome.

As many as a million people (probably 1 in 5 of the Gauls) died, another million were enslaved, 300 tribes were subjugated and 800 cities were destroyed during the Gallic Wars. The entire population of the city of Avaricum (Bourges) (40,000 in all) were slaughtered.[10] During Julius Caesar's campaign against the Helvetii (present-day Switzerland) approximately 60% of the tribe was destroyed, and another 20% was taken into slavery. resulting in the expansion of the Roman Republic over the whole of Gaul, and pushing their northern border to the river Rhine. 7
